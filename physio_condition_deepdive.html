What Causes Clubfoot?
Etiology & Risk Factors
✨ Explain Simply
The exact etiology of Congenital Talipes Equinovarus (CTEV) remains multifactorial. Current evidence supports a combination of genetic predisposition, arrested fetal development, and intrauterine mechanical factors acting during the critical period of musculoskeletal development (7–8 weeks gestation).
✨
AI Simple Explanation
Think of it this way — during early pregnancy, the baby's foot is forming. Clubfoot happens when something interrupts the normal development process. It could be genes passed down from parents, the baby's foot getting stuck in an awkward position inside the womb, or the muscles and tendons pulling unevenly. Most of the time (80%), we don't find a single clear cause — we call that idiopathic, which just means "we know it happened, but not exactly why." The key thing to understand clinically is: it's not the parents' fault, and it's very treatable.
🧬
Genetic / Hereditary
Positive family history increases risk 30x. Associated with chromosome 17q23 locus. Autosomal dominant pattern in some families.
🤰
Intrauterine Factors
Oligohydramnios, abnormal fetal positioning, and restricted movement in utero compress the developing foot.
🧠
Neuromuscular Theory
Imbalance between invertor/evertor muscles leads to abnormal tendon insertion and secondary bone deformity.
⏱️
Arrested Development
Normal foot passes through an equinovarus position at 7–8 weeks gestation. Arrest at this stage = clubfoot.

The 4-Component Deformity
Remember: CAVE (Cavus → Adductus → Varus → Equinus)
✨ Explain Simply
Clubfoot is a complex 3-dimensional deformity involving all structures of the foot and ankle. It is best understood using the mnemonic CAVE, describing the four components in order from forefoot to hindfoot.
✨
AI Simple Explanation
Imagine trying to describe a twisted foot — instead of memorising four separate things, just remember CAVE. Start at the front of the foot and work backwards: the arch is too high (Cavus), the front half curves inward like a kidney (Adductus), the heel rolls inward (Varus), and the whole foot points downward like a ballet dancer (Equinus). In your exam, if they show you a clubfoot X-ray or ask you to assess one clinically — CAVE tells you exactly what to look for and in what order.
🔴 C — Cavus (High Arch)
🟣 A — Adductus (Forefoot)
🟢 V — Varus (Heel)
🟡 E — Equinus (Ankle)
💡 Clinical Pearl
The deformity is rigid in true CTEV — unlike positional clubfoot which is flexible and corrects easily with gentle manipulation. This distinction is your first clinical assessment step.

Clinical Presentation
What you'll see in real practice
✨ Explain Simply
On clinical examination, the foot presents with a characteristic appearance. Inspection reveals a small foot with skin creases on the medial border and posterior heel. The calf is hypoplastic and the foot lies in the classic CAVE position. Palpation reveals tight posteromedial structures including the Achilles tendon, tibialis posterior, and FHL.
✨
AI Simple Explanation
When a baby with clubfoot is placed in front of you, here's what to look for step by step. First — look: the foot points downward and inward, the heel is pulled up, and there are deep skin folds on the inside and back of the foot. The calf muscle on that side is thinner than the other. Second — feel: the structures on the inner side (tibialis posterior) and back of the foot (Achilles) feel very tight and rope-like. Third — move: you won't be able to bring the foot into a normal position — that rigidity is the key sign separating true CTEV from a postural deformity.
⚠️
Pirani Scoring System
Assess severity — guides treatment planning
✨ Explain Simply
Hindfoot Score (0–3)
• Posterior crease
• Empty heel
• Rigid equinus
Midfoot Score (0–3)
• Medial crease
• Curved lateral border
• Lateral head of talus
Total Score 0–6: Higher score = more severe deformity = more casts needed before tenotomy

Ponseti Method — Gold Standard
95%+ success rate when started early
✨ Explain Simply
The Ponseti technique is the internationally accepted gold-standard treatment for idiopathic CTEV. It exploits the plasticity of neonatal cartilage and ligamentous tissue to achieve progressive correction through serial manipulation and casting.
✨
AI Simple Explanation
The Ponseti method is brilliant because it works with the baby's biology. Newborn cartilage and ligaments are soft and moldable — like warm clay. Each week, the physiotherapist/orthopaedist gently stretches the foot a little further toward normal position and applies a plaster cast to hold it there. After 5–7 weeks, the foot is mostly corrected. The Achilles is still tight though — so a tiny cut is made (tenotomy) to release it. Then comes the most important part that parents often struggle with: the brace worn 23 hours/day for 3 months, then at night for 4 years. Skipping the brace is the #1 cause of relapse.
1
Serial Manipulation & Casting
Weekly gentle manipulation correcting CAVE in sequence (Cavus first, then Adductus/Varus, then Equinus). Above-knee casts applied after each session. Average 5–7 casts required.
⏱ Weeks 1–7
2
Percutaneous Achilles Tenotomy
Performed in 90% of cases when equinus remains after casting. Simple office procedure under local anaesthesia. Final cast applied for 3 weeks post-tenotomy.
🔪 Minor procedure
3
Foot Abduction Brace (FAB)
Denis Browne bar with open-toed shoes at 60–70° external rotation. 23 hr/day for 3 months, then 12–14 hrs/night until age 4–5 years. Compliance is critical.
⭐ Most critical step
4
Physiotherapy & Exercises
Parent education for stretching, stimulation exercises. Monitoring of gait development, muscle strength, and tibialis anterior activity as child grows.
👨‍⚕️ Ongoing
